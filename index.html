Procedural Aesthetics:

Emerging Styles by Ensembling Procedurally-Anchored Models

Generating Procedural Aesthetics - Emerging Styles without Art Datasets

This gallery presents flower images that look painted, printed, stitched, or carved - yet the system that generated them was never trained on paintings, illustrations, or any art dataset.

Research Questions:

(1) How much can a text‑to‑image model learn by procedural filters to generate a recognizable visual art style without ever being trained on artwork samples?

(2) Can the ensemble of several models trained with procedural filters of different art style create some emerging, yet visually coherent art styles? ⤵

To explore this, I trained a diffusion-based text-to-image model entirely from scratch using only real flower photographs (Oxford Flower-102). No pre-trained checkpoints. No inherited aesthetic priors. Any “artistic” qualities here are not copied from art history; they are grown through constraint.

The method is called procedural style anchoring. After learning to generate flowers from text, the model is fine-tuned on the same photographs after they are transformed by deterministic procedural filters—posterization, halftone logic, pointillist sampling, mosaic tiling, cross-hatching fields, low-poly abstraction, anisotropic Kuwahara smoothing, and more. These procedures act like a kind of visual physics: they impose consistent rules about edges, texture, and color, and the model internalizes those rules as a stable aesthetic.

The highlight of this work—and the engine of its creative surprise—is ensembling. Instead of relying on one style model at a time, I combine multiple procedurally anchored models during generation using weighted soft blending, sometimes block-wise across the network. Different “style instincts” can influence different parts of the diffusion process, allowing hybrid aesthetics to emerge: halftone structure with fauvist color, mosaic geometry with painterly softness, felt-like texture fused with poster-flat tonal fields. The result is not a simple average, but a negotiation—an interference pattern where new visual dialects appear between established styles.

"Can a model grow a recognizable “art style” without learning from human artworks—purely from rules, constraints, and the dynamics of generation? Browse slowly. Some images demonstrate single anchored styles; others embrace the ensemble hybrids where the most unexpected forms bloom. Let the textures, edges, and color decisions hold your attention—marks that were never painted, only discovered."

Proposed Technical Implementation

Here, the technical implementation involving data collection, model selection and training is described... ⤵

Data Collection and Preprocessing

Dataset is constructed from:

  • Corrupt Image and Video Data: Scraped from archives of compressed, degraded, or intentionally distorted media.
  • Glitches: Generated by applying algorithmic distortions such as pixel sorting, color channel shifts, and noise-based corruption techniques.

Each data sample undergoes preprocessing steps, including normalization, resizing, and structured augmentation to introduce controlled levels of randomness while preserving underlying glitch features. Data augmentation methods such as random cropping, spectral decomposition, and edge degradation are applied to simulate natural error propagation in digital media.


Model Selection and Training Pipeline

A Diffusion Model is employed to synthesize glitch-inspired visuals. The model architecture follows a multi-stage process:

  • Feature Extraction: A pre-trained model extracts low- and high-frequency features from the dataset to inform glitch-based textures.

Generator and Discriminator Design:

  • The generator is conditioned on noise vectors and input embeddings derived from the extracted features, allowing it to reconstruct and amplify glitch patterns.
  • The discriminator employs a combination of perceptual loss and adversarial training to differentiate between human-created and AI-generated glitch artifacts.

Training Strategy:

  • The model is trained over multiple epochs with progressively increasing corruption parameters to reinforce glitch characteristics.
  • Fine-tuning is carried out with transfer learning techniques using a secondary dataset consisting of abstract art and photography.
